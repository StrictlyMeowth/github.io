Orientation & Legend

Quick-reference glossary for understanding the AI landscape. Memo-style, not a lesson.
Entity Types

Organization / Company

A corporation or entity that builds, funds, or ships AI. Some are pure AI companies (OpenAI, Anthropic). Others are tech giants with AI divisions (Google, Microsoft, Meta). The distinction matters because it tells you who controls the money, the data, and the strategy.

AI Lab

A team focused on AI research and model development. May be standalone (Anthropic, DeepSeek) or a unit inside a larger company (Google DeepMind inside Alphabet). "Lab" implies research focus, not just product shipping. Not every AI company is a lab.

Model

The trained AI system itself — the "brain." A model processes inputs and generates outputs. Models are named in families (GPT-5.x, Claude 4.x, Gemini 3.x). You never interact with a model directly — you use it through an app, an API, or a platform.

App / Tool

The product you actually use. ChatGPT is an app; GPT-5.4 is the model behind it. Claude.ai is an app; Claude Sonnet 4.6 is a model. Apps wrap models with a user interface, safety features, memory, file handling, and other product decisions.

Platform / Infrastructure

The services developers use to build with AI models. Cloud hosting (AWS Bedrock, Google Vertex AI), model serving, fine-tuning. You don't use these directly unless you're building something. They power the apps you do use.

Protocol / Orchestration

Standards and tools that help AI models talk to other software. MCP (Model Context Protocol) lets models connect to external tools. LangGraph helps chain AI steps together. These are plumbing — invisible but increasingly important.

Model Tiers & Types

Flagship / Frontier

The most capable model a lab offers. Maximum intelligence, highest cost. Examples: GPT-5.4, Claude Opus 4.6, Gemini 3.1 Pro. Used for hard tasks where quality beats speed.

Balanced / Mid-Tier

Strong performance with reasonable speed and cost. The workhorse for most tasks. Examples: Claude Sonnet 4.6, Gemini 3.1 Flash. What most people should use most of the time.

Lightweight / Fast

Smaller, cheaper, faster. Good enough for routine tasks. Examples: GPT-5.4 mini/nano, Claude Haiku 4.5, Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite. Used in high-volume apps or as "sub-agents" doing simple work inside a larger system.

Reasoning Model

A model that "thinks step by step" before answering, spending extra time on hard problems. Visible as a "thinking" mode in apps. Slower but more accurate on math, logic, coding, and complex analysis. Examples: GPT-5.4 Thinking, DeepSeek-R1.

Coding Model

Optimized for writing, editing, and reviewing code. May be a separate model (GPT-5.3-Codex) or a mode of a general model. Powers tools like Cursor, GitHub Copilot, and Codex.

Media Model (Image / Video / Audio)

Generates or manipulates images, video, or audio. Examples: FLUX.1 (images), Veo 3.1 (video), Suno (music). Often a separate model architecture from text/reasoning models.

Access & Licensing

Open-Weight

The model's trained parameters are publicly downloadable. Anyone can run it, modify it, or build on it. Examples: Llama 4, DeepSeek-V3.2, Kimi K2.5. "Open-weight" ≠ "open-source" — the training code/data may still be private.

Proprietary

The model is only accessible through the company's own app or API. You can't download or inspect it. Examples: GPT-5.4, Claude Opus 4.6, Gemini 3.1 Pro. Most frontier models are proprietary.

API

Application Programming Interface. A way for developers to send prompts to a model and get responses back programmatically. How most AI products are built — they call a model's API behind the scenes.

Protocols / Orchestration / Agent Tooling
Model Context Protocol (MCP) Protocol
Open standard created by Anthropic for connecting AI models to external tools and data sources. Think of it as a universal plug that lets any AI model talk to any application (databases, APIs, files, services). Rapidly adopted across the industry — now supported by OpenAI, Google, and many tools.
Why it matters: Becoming the standard way AI tools connect to the world
Who uses it: Developers, tool builders, AI platforms
modelcontextprotocol.io →
LangGraph Orchestration
Framework for building complex AI agent workflows as graphs. Part of the LangChain ecosystem. Lets developers create multi-step, branching AI pipelines with state management. The most popular agent orchestration framework.
Who uses it: Developers building complex AI agent systems

Debugging and monitoring platform for AI applications. Trace, evaluate, and improve LLM-powered apps. Part of the LangChain ecosystem. Helps developers understand why their AI app behaves the way it does.
